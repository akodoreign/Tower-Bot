THE FALL OF THE EASTERN MARCH

Being a True Account of the Orcish Ascendancy, the Ruin of the Imperial
Lowlands, and the End of Sir Alard Montreval

Compiled from garrison field reports, survivor testimony, military census
records, and the oral histories of the Reval hill settlements

Grand Forum Archive · Restricted Circulation

"He came back. He held the wall. He delayed the inevitable by a year, and
because of it perhaps a thousand people who would otherwise have died in
the first week did not. The historians who wish to stop the story there are
welcome to do so. The rest of us have to account for the second year."

— Archivist's preface, first edition

CHAPTER ONE

The Year of the Returned Commander

The war did not begin when the wall fell. It began when the Empire decided
the wall was enough.

For three seasons before the invasion, the border garrison of the Eastern
March operated in a condition that its own supply manifests described as
adequate and that its own soldiers described, in private, as hollowed out.
The commander who had understood the threat — who had documented it, mapped
it, and pressed it up through the chain of rank until the chain of rank
stopped returning his correspondence — had been removed from his post and
sent to a village in the Reval hill country. His name was Sir Alard
Montreval. His removal was one of the most consequential administrative
decisions the Empire ever made.

Montreval had not been wrong. He had been inconvenient, which in the
Imperial command structure amounted to the same charge with a different
punishment. He had argued, with evidence, that the orcish confederacy to
the north was not the disorganised raiding culture that three generations
of Imperial doctrine described. He had argued they were building something.
That they were patient. That the probing skirmishes along the border were
not opportunism but reconnaissance, and that the soldiers conducting them
were learning exactly what the Eastern March could and could not withstand.

The Empire thanked him for his service and relieved him of command.

In the year that followed his dismissal, the garrison continued its
patrols. The supply manifests continued to report adequacy. The probing
skirmishes continued on a rhythm that border soldiers learned to anticipate
the way farmers learn to anticipate weather. A succession of officers
rotated through the Eastern March command, each inheriting the
institutional certainty that the threat was manageable, each departing
before the consequences of that certainty had time to arrive.

One thing happened in that year that would prove more significant than any
of the command decisions: in the mountain passes above the Reval Heights,
where the only crossing between the border country and the lower valley
settlements had long been a deteriorating log bridge across a river gap, a
stone causeway was laid. The archive has found no record of who built it or
why. The hill settlements remember it being constructed by a small group of
travellers passing through — people moving with clear purpose who stayed
long enough to finish the job properly and then left without ceremony.

At the time this was noted only by those nearby, who used the path
gratefully when occasion required. Its significance would not become clear
until the wall fell.

Sir Alard Montreval returned to command in the late autumn of that year.

He did not demand reinstatement. He was asked for it. The same chain of
rank that had dismissed him quietly reversed itself when the orcish
confederacy stopped its probing skirmishes along the border — a development
that every other officer in the Eastern March interpreted as a sign of
reduced threat, and that Montreval had always maintained was the most
alarming signal possible. When they stopped probing, he had written, three
years before his dismissal, it would be because they already knew
everything they needed to know.

He returned to the Eastern March without comment on what had been done to
him. He surveyed what remained of the garrison's readiness with the
expression of a man who had expected exactly what he found. He began to
prepare a defence with the material available to him, which was not
sufficient, and which he knew was not sufficient, and which he prepared
anyway because the alternative was to prepare nothing.

Under his command, the wall held for eleven months.

This is not a small thing. Every qualified assessment the archive has
gathered from officers who were present, from soldiers who served in that
period, from the supply records that tracked what was consumed and what was
replaced, agrees that the Eastern March under Montreval's returned command
lasted far longer than it had any material right to last. He positioned
undermanned defences with the precision of someone who had memorised every
angle of that fortification better than the architects had. He maintained
morale in soldiers who had been given years of reasons to abandon it. He
drilled. He organised. He held what the Empire had already decided could
not be held, and he held it for eleven months, and the eleven months
mattered. They mattered to the civilians in the lowland provinces who used
those months to move. They mattered to the settlements in the hill country
who used those months to prepare. The archive estimates, conservatively,
that the eleven months of the wall's extended resistance under Montreval
saved several thousand lives that would otherwise have been lost in the
opening weeks of the campaign.

It was not enough. The wall fell in the twelfth month, and everything that
follows in this account is what insufficient looks like when it is all that
was ever available.

CHAPTER TWO

The Walls Come Down

The assault on the Eastern March came on a morning in the second year of
Montreval's returned command, and it was everything he had spent three
years warning the Empire about.

It came from two directions simultaneously.

The primary force struck from the north — thousands strong, coordinated in
a way that Imperial doctrine had long insisted the confederacy could not
sustain. They came not in the massed frontal rush the garrison's defensive
architecture had been built to break, but in measured overlapping waves
with clear tactical intent, each wave covering the withdrawal of the last,
each probing a different section of the wall's firing lines. The soldiers
on the eastern battlements said afterward that fighting them felt less like
repelling an assault and more like being read by someone who had long since
memorised the relevant passages.

The secondary force came through the Ashfell Ravine.

Three separate Imperial surveys had assessed the Ashfell as impassable for
organised military formations. The surveys were not wrong about the natural
terrain. The orcish engineers had spent a full season removing the relevant
obstacles. By the morning of the assault the ravine was a prepared route,
and the formation moving through it was light, fast, and specifically
selected for the crossing. They emerged behind the wall while the
garrison's full attention was committed forward.

Montreval recognised what was happening within the first hour. Officers who
survived describe him giving the order to collapse the forward positions
and consolidate on the inner wall with the calm of a man who had rehearsed
this possibility until it felt, in its arrival, like a repetition of
something already completed.

The inner wall bought six more hours. Six hours beyond what a garrison of
that size defending a double line had any right to produce. Montreval
positioned the remaining soldiers with the precision of someone who had
memorised the defensive geometry of every firing angle and every dead
ground approach, and the soldiers held because he told them where to stand
and because, by that point, they believed him about things.

The wall fell in the evening.

The survivors broke for the Reval Heights. Soldiers and the civilian
population of the border hamlet, moving together in the dark toward the
mountain crossing, drove toward the stone path that the travellers had laid
across the Reval Gap the previous year. The original log bridge was already
gone — spring rain had been sufficient to finish what years of neglect had
begun. The stone causeway held. It held several hundred soldiers and their
equipment. It held civilians with carts and children and everything they
had been able to carry. It held under the full weight of everyone who
reached it and it continued to hold.

Approximately nine hundred people crossed the Reval Heights that night. The
archive's careful estimate is that without the stone causeway, the number
would have been fewer than three hundred.

Montreval was not among the nine hundred.

From the testimony of Sergeant Erya Voss, Eastern March garrison: "He was
at the rear of the column for the entire retreat. We kept trying to move
him forward and he kept sending us forward instead and staying back
himself. The last time I saw him he was at the base of the Heights road
still directing people up toward the path. The column closed between us. I
did not see him after that."

CHAPTER THREE

The Stone Path and What Followed It

The causeway that saved the garrison's survivors also let the enemy
through. This is stated plainly because to state it otherwise would be
dishonest.

The orcish advance column reached the Reval Heights on the second day of
the campaign. The log bridge was gone. The stone causeway was intact. There
was nothing remarkable about this — it had been built well, and it behaved
as well-built stone behaves. The advance column crossed without difficulty
and reached the hamlet beyond the Heights by afternoon.

The settlement called Reval Cross by its inhabitants had absorbed a
significant number of refugees from the border regions in the preceding
days and had not yet evacuated its own permanent residents. The debate
about whether to leave had the quality of all such debates in small
communities: earnest, prolonged, and overtaken by events before a decision
was reached. The advance column arrived on the second afternoon. The
settlement's population, at last census, was four hundred and twelve.

The archive records Reval Cross as lost.

There is a letter in the archive, author unknown, recovered from a Reval
Heights farmstead some months after the campaign:

"The same stones. We crossed on them and they crossed on them and I do not
know how to hold both of those things at once. I have been told it was not
wrong to build the path. I believe this most of the time. I cannot stop
turning it over."

The archive does not adjudicate this. The path was built in good faith by
people who could not have known what year would follow. It served the
purpose it was built for. It was then used for another purpose entirely.
These three facts exist together without resolving into a lesson, which is
perhaps the most honest thing that can be said about them.

CHAPTER FOUR

The March on the Lowlands

The orcish confederacy did not stop at the Eastern March. The wall was not
the point. The wall was the door.

The lowland provinces were not prepared. They had been told, consistently
and with confidence, that the Eastern March was holding. When the advance
columns of the confederacy appeared on roads where no enemy had walked in
living memory, the response was, with honourable exceptions, one of genuine
and total surprise.

The Trevaine Breadlands fell in the first week. The river settlements of
the Ossal proved more difficult — several communities held their crossing
points for days beyond what the strategic situation warranted, buying time
for civilians to move south at the cost of those who stayed. Their names
are in the Merchant Consortium's memorial records. They are not in Imperial
records.

The fortress-towns fell one by one through the following weeks. One,
Harstwall on the eastern fork of the Ossal, never formally surrendered. The
confederacy walked around it. What became of Harstwall in the long
occupation is not established.

The campaign moved south with the patience of something planned over years
rather than improvised over weeks. Town by town, valley by valley, the
lowlands that had been the heart of the Empire for three centuries became,
within forty days, occupied territory. And leading the advance — preceding
it, defining it, making it impossible to witness without understanding
exactly what it was saying — was the thing that Vexrath Sablegear had
built.

CHAPTER FIVE

Sablegear's Machine

Vexrath Sablegear did not fight in the lowland campaign. He built.

The black dragonborn engineer who had overseen Project Blackwake — the
inland warship, the proof of orcish strategic ambition that the Empire had
been warned about repeatedly and had repeatedly declined to act upon — had
not abandoned his work when the wall fell. He had expanded its scope. What
emerged was a mobile fortification. A war platform. A deliberate argument
made in iron and salvaged stone.

It incorporated the completed hull sections and iron rigging of the
Blackwake vessel, dragged overland at enormous cost in labour. To this core
Sablegear added salvaged wall sections from the Eastern March itself. Dock
crane mechanisms stripped from the river port at Reval Bay. Load-bearing
timbers from the garrison barracks. Ironwork stripped from the border gate.
He built the machine out of the Empire's own bones, and he built it while
moving.

The machine moved slowly and arrived at settlements before they had
finished arguing about whether to hold or run. It was not the fastest
element of the advance. It was the most visible. And mounted at its forward
aspect, between two iron frames that had been designed specifically for the
purpose, was a man.

Sir Alard Montreval had been captured at the base of the Reval Heights on
the night of the wall's fall. He had stayed at the rear of the retreat
until the last moment, directing people up toward the causeway, and the
confederacy's flanking units had closed around him before he reached the
path himself.

He was taken alive, which was not the confederacy's standard practice, and
the deliberateness of this was itself a message. Montreval had been right
about the threat. He had warned the Empire for years. He had been
dismissed, recalled when his warnings became undeniable, and then held the
wall for eleven months against the opponent he had always understood better
than anyone above him. Sablegear wanted the Empire to see its disgraced,
reinstated, ultimately insufficient commander. He wanted to make that
visibility permanent. He wanted it moving at the front of everything the
machine was doing.

Montreval was mounted on the forward frame of the machine and carried south
through every province the advance crossed.

He watched the Breadlands go. He watched the river towns go. He watched
the fortress-towns hold longer than they should have and then fall anyway.
He watched the roads he had patrolled as a young officer carry an army he
had spent his career trying to prevent. He was kept alive throughout. He
faced forward. He was positioned so that everything the machine moved
toward was in his direct sight, and everyone who saw the machine coming
understood — before they could see anything else clearly — who was at the
front of it and what that meant.

From an account recovered at Crossharbour, author unidentified: "We saw
him before we saw the machine. From the walls, when it was still too
distant to make out the full shape, there was a figure at the front. We
knew who it was before anyone said the name. We knew from how he was held,
how he couldn't move, how the whole thing was arranged so that you would
see him first. We knew, and then we saw what was carrying him, and we
understood what both of them were there to say."

CHAPTER SIX

The Capital

The Imperial capital had not been successfully besieged in two hundred
years. Its walls were structurally sound. Its garrison, even depleted, was
larger than anything that had held at the border. The city had supplies for
a long siege.

The walls did not fail. The belief in the walls failed first.

Sablegear's machine drew up before the main gate at dawn on the forty-third
day of the campaign. No attack was launched immediately. No terms were
offered. The machine simply stopped there, with Montreval visible at its
forward frame to any defender who looked toward the gate. Every defender
looked. They saw a man who had been right about everything, held upright
and alive by the thing that had proven him right, positioned to watch what
was about to happen to the city he had spent his career defending. They saw
the machine built from the bones of every fortification that had already
failed. And they understood what patience looks like when it has finished
waiting.

The gate opened before midday.

Sablegear entered the capital at midday. The machine followed him through
the gate.

Montreval was still alive on the forward frame as the machine passed
through the gate. He was still alive as it moved through the inner
districts. He was still alive when it reached the Imperial administrative
quarter and stopped. And he was screaming.

Not from pain, though pain was present. Not for help, which was not coming.
Every survivor account agrees on the quality of the sound: it was not the
screaming of a man who had broken. It was the screaming of a man who had
decided that bearing witness was the only thing left available to him, and
who was doing it at full volume. He screamed the names of streets he
recognised. He screamed the names of buildings. He screamed at the machine
itself, which did not answer. He screamed at Sablegear, who did not turn to
look at him. He screamed at the city as it was unmade around him, using the
voice that had commanded soldiers across eleven months of the hardest
defensive action in the Eastern March's history, spending it now on pure
documentation of what he was being made to see.

He was taken down from the forward frame when the machine stopped. He was
alive. The archive does not have adequate testimony to describe his state
beyond that fact. He disappears from the record at that point.

CHAPTER SEVEN

The Consortium Holds, and What Was Already Gone

The line held at the Crossharbour reach. It was not held by the Empire.

The Merchant Consortium of the Northern Reaches assembled a relief force in
fourteen days — the largest private military expenditure the region had
seen in living memory. They took the Crossharbour defensive line and held
it when the confederacy struck, nine days of fighting after which the
confederacy withdrew to consolidate. The advance stopped at Crossharbour
and did not resume.

What had been lost by then was vast. The Eastern March was gone entirely.
The Trevaine Breadlands were producing grain for a different
administration. The river towns were occupied. The fortress-towns that had
held were now garrisoned by soldiers who had not built them. The Imperial
capital stood, occupied.

The stone causeway across the Reval Gap stood as well. It stands as of
this writing. No marker names who built it. The hill settlements nearby
know only what they saw: a small group of travellers came through in the
year before the war. They built the crossing. They left when it was done.
Whether they knew what the year after their departure would bring, no one
can say.

ARCHIVIST'S CLOSING NOTE

The archive has been asked whether the outcome was inevitable. Whether the
wall better supplied, the commander reinstated sooner, a second prepared
line of defence in the lowland provinces, would have changed what this text
describes. Whether the eleven months Montreval bought at the Eastern March
were sufficient foundation for something that simply never got built on top
of them.

The archive does not answer this. Its function is to record what happened,
not to revise it.

What happened is here. The reader may draw their own conclusions about the
distance between a thing being delayed and a thing being prevented. The
archive notes only: the wall was held for eleven months by a man who spent
three years being told the threat was not real. The stone path across the
Reval Gap was built by persons unknown in the year before the war. Nine
hundred people crossed it on the night the wall fell. These are facts. They
do not redeem the outcome. They are not nothing.

On Sir Alard Montreval: No verified account of his fate after the
occupation of the capital has been located. The archive records his status
as unknown and continues to receive testimony.

On Vexrath Sablegear: The machine was partially disassembled following the
capital's fall. Sablegear's whereabouts after the end of the campaign are
not established by any source the archive considers reliable.

On the stone causeway: Still standing. Well-maintained by the hill
settlements. Assessed by Consortium engineers who described it as built by
someone who understood mountain drainage and expected the path to outlast
them. No name is recorded. The local settlements were asked. They said:
travellers came through. They built it. They left. That is all anyone knows.
